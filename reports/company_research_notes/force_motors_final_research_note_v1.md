## 1. Final investment-style conclusion

Force Motors is an improving Indian automobile company with strong exposure to vans, shared mobility, specialized mobility, utility vehicles, commercial vehicles and contract engine manufacturing for premium OEMs. FY25 and 9M FY26 results show very strong profitability improvement, supported by operating leverage, demand for Urbania/Traveller/Gurkha/Trax/Monobus platforms, domestic volume growth, and sharply lower finance costs. The company also reported nil total debt in Q3 FY26 based on its official press-release summary.

However, Force Motors remains a cyclical auto company and its recent profitability contains both operating leverage and exceptional/one-off items. Therefore, it should not be valued simply by annualizing Q3 FY26 earnings or applying a low PE multiple to peak profits. A normalized cyclical DCF is more appropriate.

Based on final v1 DCF assumptions and a current price proxy of approximately Rs 22,000 per share, Force Motors does not qualify as a base-case margin-of-safety candidate under a 35% required MOS. The bull case is attractive but depends on sustained demand, high utilization, margin durability and strong cash-flow conversion.

### Final classification v1

> Watchlist / Cyclical Auto Growth Candidate — Not a base-case margin-of-safety candidate at current price. Attractive only if purchased with a large margin of safety or if the analyst has high conviction that the recent margin step-up is structurally sustainable.

## 3. Business overview

Force Motors manufactures light commercial vehicles, utility vehicles, vans, passenger carriers, specialized vehicles and engines. The company is known for platforms such as Traveller, Urbania, Trax, Gurkha and Monobus. It also manufactures engines for premium passenger vehicle OEMs in India.

Key business drivers:

1. Domestic commercial and passenger carrier demand
2. School bus, ambulance and shared mobility demand
3. Urbania and Traveller platform growth
4. Utility vehicle demand, including Gurkha
5. Contract engine manufacturing volumes
6. Exports to emerging markets
7. Operating leverage and cost control
8. Finance-cost reduction / debt-free balance sheet
9. Government incentives and exceptional items

Key strengths:

- niche leadership in vans/passenger carriers
- integrated manufacturing capability
- premium OEM engine manufacturing exposure
- strong FY26 operating leverage
- debt-free position in Q3 FY26 official summary

Key limitations:

- cyclical auto demand
- lower scale than larger auto OEMs
- dependence on platform success
- margin sensitivity to volume and product mix
- exceptional items can distort reported PAT

## 4. FY25 and Q4 FY25 context

Force Motors reported a strong Q4 FY25 profit increase. Reuters reported that Q4 net profit more than tripled, helped by strong sales of multi-seater vans and a one-time government incentive gain. Excluding exceptional items, pre-tax profit still rose meaningfully, but the result shows why analysts must separate operating improvement from exceptional income.

Q4 FY25 key context:

| Item | Observation |
|---|---|
| Net profit | more than tripled YoY in Reuters report |
| Revenue growth | about 17% YoY in Reuters report |
| Driver | demand for multi-seater vans such as school buses and ambulances |
| One-off item | government incentive gain materially boosted profit |
| Important lesson | reported PAT should be normalized before DCF |

Interpretation:

- Demand momentum was real.
- Exceptional items also contributed meaningfully.
- Normalized FCFF must be lower than peak reported profit if one-off gains are not recurring.

## 6. Exceptional-item and normalization review

Force Motors' Q3 FY26 result included exceptional items related to government incentives and labour-code expenses. Public reports indicate exceptional income from government incentives, partly offset by labour-code-related expenses.

Why this matters:

- PAT after exceptional items overstates recurring profitability if incentives are non-recurring.
- PBT before exceptional items is more useful for base-case valuation.
- A DCF should normalize earnings and FCFF across the auto cycle.

DCF treatment:

| Scenario | Current FCFF proxy | Reasoning |
|---|---:|---|
| Bear | Rs 450 crore | assumes margins normalize and exceptional gains fade |
| Base | Rs 900 crore | assumes strong operations sustain partly, but not peak PAT |
| Bull | Rs 1,350 crore | assumes Q3/Q9M margin strength sustains and cash conversion is strong |

## 13. Literature-based review

### Damodaran lens

- A cyclical auto company should be valued using normalized cash flows.
- Exceptional items must not be treated as recurring FCFF.
- FCFF must incorporate capex and working-capital needs, not just PAT.

### Fisher lens

- Force has a differentiated niche in passenger carriers and specialized mobility.
- The key qualitative question is whether management can scale Urbania/Traveller and engine relationships sustainably.
- Customer and product-level checks would improve confidence.

### Basant Maheshwari / Indian market lens

- Strong earnings growth can create re-rating, but buying after re-rating can be risky.
- Auto cyclicals need entry-price discipline.
- Debt-free balance sheet improves quality but does not remove cycle risk.

### Malkiel lens

- Avoid extrapolating recent profit momentum.
- Compare active cyclical bet against broad-market alternatives.
- Maintain humility around forecasting auto demand and margins.

### Palomar / portfolio lens

- Force Motors adds auto-cycle exposure and may correlate with other auto names like Hero and Fiem.
- Position size should reflect concentration risk in the auto theme.

### Stochastic DCF lens

- Scenario analysis is essential because cash flows depend on uncertain volume, margin and working-capital outcomes.
- A future Monte Carlo model could vary EBITDA margin, volume growth and capex intensity.

## 15. Final conclusion v1

Force Motors has delivered a strong FY26 performance with improving revenue, record margins, large PAT growth and a debt-free Q3 FY26 position. Its niche vehicle platforms and engine manufacturing relationships make it an interesting auto research candidate.

However, the current valuation appears to require sustained margin strength and strong cash-flow conversion. The base-case DCF does not support a 35% margin of safety at the current price proxy. The bull case is attractive, but the analyst should wait for FY26 audited annual report, full cash-flow reconciliation and evidence that margins are structurally higher rather than temporarily elevated.

### Final view

> Watchlist / Cyclical Auto Growth Candidate — Not a base-case margin-of-safety candidate at current price. Revisit after FY26 audited results, exceptional-item reconciliation, full-year CFO/capex and updated platform-demand outlook.
