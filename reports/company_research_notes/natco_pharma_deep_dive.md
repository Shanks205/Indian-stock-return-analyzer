## 1. Executive conclusion

Natco Pharma remains an interesting but high-uncertainty DCF candidate. The company has strong FY25 profitability, high operating cash generation, a low-debt/net-cash profile, and a visible optionality pipeline from complex generics and semaglutide. However, the business should not be valued like a stable consumer company because its revenue and profits are meaningfully affected by product launches, profit-share arrangements, regulatory approvals, patent events, and pricing cycles.

The first-pass scenario DCF shows:

| Scenario | Approx. intrinsic value per share | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Bear | ~Rs 600 | If FY25 profitability normalizes sharply lower |
| Base | ~Rs 1,075 | Close to current price proxy; no strong margin of safety |
| Bull | ~Rs 1,750 | Attractive only if pipeline execution sustains growth |

Final provisional view:

> Natco Pharma is a watchlist / deeper-research candidate, not a clear margin-of-safety candidate under the base case. The stock becomes interesting only if the analyst has high conviction in sustained complex-generic cash flows and semaglutide/agrochemical growth.

## 2. Business overview

Natco develops, manufactures, and distributes generic and branded pharmaceuticals, specialty pharmaceuticals, active pharmaceutical ingredients, and crop protection products. The company is R&D-oriented, with a focus on oncology and limited-competition molecules. It caters to 50+ global markets and has facilities approved by regulators such as US FDA, Brazil ANVISA, Health Canada, and WHO.

Main operating areas:

1. Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients (API)
2. Domestic formulations
3. Formulations exports, including profit-share and subsidiaries
4. Crop Health Sciences
5. Strategic investments / associated company contribution

## 3. Latest quarterly performance — Q3 FY26

Natco reported Q3 FY26 consolidated total revenue of Rs 705.4 crore versus Rs 651.1 crore in Q3 FY25. EBITDA including other income was Rs 216.8 crore, with EBITDA margin of 30.7%. Consolidated profit after tax was Rs 151.3 crore.

Segment split for Q3 FY26:

| Segment | Q3 FY26 revenue | Q2 FY26 revenue | Q3 FY25 revenue | Observation |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| API | Rs 64.3 crore | Rs 53.9 crore | Rs 66.6 crore | Stable / modest |
| Domestic formulations | Rs 119.8 crore | Rs 105.4 crore | Rs 96.1 crore | Improving domestic base |
| Formulations export | Rs 421.4 crore | Rs 1,147.0 crore | Rs 285.8 crore | Very volatile; key risk area |
| Crop Health Sciences | Rs 28.5 crore | Rs 52.4 crore | Rs 15.1 crore | Small but variable |
| Other income | Rs 71.3 crore | Rs 104.3 crore | Rs 187.5 crore | Other income volatility matters |
| Total | Rs 705.4 crore | Rs 1,463.0 crore | Rs 651.1 crore | Sequential volatility is large |

Analyst interpretation:

The Q3 data shows why a simple straight-line DCF can be dangerous for Natco. Q2 FY26 revenue was much higher than Q3 FY26, mainly because export formulations can swing sharply. The base-case DCF should therefore use normalized FCFF rather than extrapolating the best quarter.

## 4. FY25 annual-report-level observations

FY25 appears to be an exceptional year for Natco. Public annual-report summaries show strong revenue, EBITDA, and profit after tax. The annual-report overview also indicates continued R&D investment, with R&D spend of around Rs 3,733 million in FY25, representing around 9.1% of sales.

Key FY25 implications:

- Strong profitability supports the base-case valuation.
- Strong operating cash flow supports DCF reliability.
- Large R&D spend supports complex-generic and specialty optionality.
- However, high profitability may include launch/product-cycle effects and should not be assumed permanent without segment-level validation.

## 5. Pipeline and growth-driver review

### 5.1 Complex generics and limited-competition launches

Natco's investment case depends heavily on its ability to develop and commercialize limited-competition products. This can create attractive profit pools, but timing is uncertain and cash flows may be uneven.

DCF implication:

- Use scenario analysis rather than one fixed growth rate.
- Avoid assuming FY25 margins continue indefinitely.
- Treat high-growth years as option-like upside rather than guaranteed base-case growth.

### 5.2 Semaglutide opportunity

Natco received CDSCO approval in February 2026 to manufacture generic semaglutide in India and launched affordable multi-dose vial formats after patent expiry in March 2026. Natco/Eris-related news suggests pricing from around Rs 1,290 per month for lower vial strengths, with pen devices priced higher. The opportunity is large because diabetes and weight-management demand is growing, but competition is also intense.

Positive implications:

- First-mover advantage in affordable multi-dose vials.
- Potential expansion of patient access because of lower pricing.
- Partnership/commercialization route can help distribution.

Risks:

- Many Indian pharma companies are entering the same molecule.
- Price competition can compress margins quickly.
- Regulatory scrutiny around weight-loss drugs is increasing.
- Demand may be large, but profitability depends on market share, manufacturing cost, and pricing durability.

DCF implication:

- Semaglutide should support bull-case assumptions more than base-case assumptions until early sales visibility improves.

### 5.3 Domestic formulations

Domestic formulations grew in Q3 FY26 versus the prior-year quarter. This business may provide a steadier foundation than export profit-share products, but it is not yet large enough to remove volatility from the overall business.

DCF implication:

- Useful for base stability.
- Not sufficient alone to justify the bull case.

### 5.4 Crop Health Sciences

Crop Health Sciences is small but potentially useful for diversification. However, agrochemical businesses can be cyclical and exposed to seasonality, channel inventory, pricing, exports, and regulatory issues.

DCF implication:

- Treat as a growth option but not a stable core cash-flow engine yet.

## 6. Cash-flow quality and normalization

For Natco, cash-flow normalization is the most important valuation step.

Key questions:

1. How much of FY25 free cash flow came from repeatable operations?
2. How much came from product-specific windfalls?
3. Are receivables and inventory rising faster than revenue?
4. Is capex recurring or expansionary?
5. Is R&D spend already expensed, or are there additional capitalized development costs?
6. Are profit-share revenues sustainable?

Current model treatment:

- Bear case uses Rs 850 crore FCFF proxy.
- Base case uses Rs 1,100 crore FCFF proxy.
- Bull case uses Rs 1,200 crore FCFF proxy.

The base case assumes FY25 cash generation is partly sustainable but does not fully extrapolate peak profits.

## 7. WACC and valuation assumptions

### Base WACC: 12.0%

Rationale:

- India equity market risk requires double-digit cost of equity.
- Pharma has regulatory and product-cycle risk.
- Natco has low financial leverage, reducing financial risk.
- Company-specific risk remains because of lumpy launches and product concentration.

### Bear WACC: 14.0%

Used when:

- pipeline disappoints,
- margins normalize lower,
- pricing pressure rises,
- product concentration risk increases.

### Bull WACC: 10.5%

Used when:

- growth visibility improves,
- cash-flow consistency improves,
- semaglutide and complex generics scale successfully,
- market assigns higher confidence to future cash flows.

### Terminal growth

- Bear: 3.5%
- Base: 4.5%
- Bull: 5.0%

Terminal growth is capped because pharma product cycles can mature and price competition can erode excess returns.

## 8. Valuation interpretation

### Bear case

The bear case assumes that FY25-level profitability fades and future FCFF grows slowly. This produces weak downside value, showing that Natco is risky if current earnings prove peak-like.

### Base case

The base case is close to current price. This means the market appears to be pricing in a reasonable amount of growth already. Under the user's margin-of-safety discipline, Natco is not a clear buy in the base case.

### Bull case

The bull case creates meaningful upside, but it depends on successful execution in complex generics, semaglutide, and broader international growth. This is not impossible, but it is not yet conservative enough to use as the central case.
