## 1. Final investment-style conclusion

Triveni Turbine is one of the highest-quality businesses in this project universe. It operates in industrial steam turbines and energy solutions, with strong exposure to industrial capex, renewable and waste-heat applications, export markets, aftermarket services, and decentralised power solutions. FY25 was a record year, and Q3 FY26 again delivered record quarterly revenue and EBITDA, supported by strong export growth and a robust order book.

However, quality does not automatically mean margin of safety. Triveni Turbine trades as a quality-growth capital-goods company, and the current market price already appears to reflect a meaningful portion of the growth story. The base-case DCF value is below the current price proxy, and the required 25% margin of safety is not present.

### Final classification v1

> Watchlist / High-Quality Capital Goods Compounder — Not a base-case margin-of-safety candidate at current price. Attractive only if purchased at a better entry price or if sustained export, aftermarket and high-margin order growth justify a higher normalized FCFF base.

### Final valuation output v1

| Scenario | Intrinsic value per share | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Bear | ~Rs 236 | Downside if growth/margins normalize and WACC rises |
| Base | ~Rs 478 | High-quality normalized value, but below current price |
| Bull | ~Rs 786 | Upside if exports, aftermarket and order conversion sustain strong growth |

Current price proxy used: Rs 580 per share.

Base-case 25% MOS buy-zone price: ~Rs 359 per share.

Because Triveni Turbine is a quality-growth capital-goods company rather than a deep cyclical commodity stock, the required margin of safety is set at 25%.

## 3. Business overview

Triveni Turbine designs, manufactures and services industrial steam turbines and related energy solutions. The company focuses on decentralised power, industrial heat and power, renewable steam-based turbines, waste-heat recovery, biomass, process co-generation and aftermarket services.

Key business areas:

1. Product turbines
2. Aftermarket services
3. Domestic industrial demand
4. Export markets
5. Renewable and waste-heat applications
6. International subsidiaries and service expansion

Key strengths:

- focused industrial turbine business
- strong technology and engineering capability
- high export contribution
- high-margin aftermarket services
- strong order book
- asset-light/high-return operating profile relative to heavy capex businesses
- debt-light / strong balance-sheet perception

Key limitations:

- capital-goods order lumpiness
- export and currency exposure
- margin sensitivity to mix
- valuation premium risk
- order-book conversion risk
- competition in global markets

## 6. Cash-flow and working-capital considerations

Capital-goods companies can report strong revenue and profit while cash flow depends on advances, milestones, receivables, inventories and execution timing. For Triveni Turbine, order booking and order backlog are very important but must ultimately convert into cash.

Key questions:

1. How much of EBITDA converts into CFO?
2. Are customer advances supporting working capital?
3. Are receivables rising with exports?
4. What is the cash conversion of aftermarket revenue?
5. Does export growth require higher working capital?
6. Does the order book convert on schedule?

DCF treatment:

| Scenario | Current FCFF proxy | Reasoning |
|---|---:|---|
| Bear | Rs 250 crore | margin normalization, slower order conversion and working-capital drag |
| Base | Rs 400 crore | strong but normalized cash generation from FY25/FY26 performance |
| Bull | Rs 550 crore | sustained export/aftermarket growth and strong cash conversion |

## 8. Growth-driver analysis

### 8.1 Export growth

Exports are one of the strongest growth drivers. FY25 export sales rose 26% and accounted for 48% of revenue. Q3 FY26 export sales grew around 54% YoY and represented around 62% of turnover.

Positive factors:

- larger global addressable market
- high-margin international orders
- diversification away from only domestic capex
- strong inquiry pipeline

Risks:

- currency volatility
- geopolitical/geographic risk
- global competition
- execution and service network requirements

DCF treatment:

- Export growth supports base-case and bull-case growth.
- Bull case requires export momentum to remain durable beyond a few quarters.

### 8.2 Aftermarket services

Aftermarket revenue was around 32% of FY25 revenue and grew 19% YoY.

Positive factors:

- recurring service opportunity
- installed-base monetization
- typically better margin quality
- less lumpy than product sales

Risks:

- service execution quality
- regional service network needs
- aftermarket mix may vary by geography

DCF treatment:

- Aftermarket improves business quality and DCF confidence.
- Sustained aftermarket mix supports higher margin assumptions.

### 8.3 Renewable and waste-heat applications

Triveni has exposure to decentralised steam-based renewable turbines, biomass, waste heat and industrial energy-efficiency solutions.

Positive factors:

- decarbonization trend
- energy-efficiency capex
- industrial sustainability demand
- global renewable/process energy opportunity

Risks:

- project delays
- competition
- policy and customer capex cycles
- technology substitution risk

DCF treatment:

- Supports long-term growth runway.
- Not all renewable narrative should be capitalized into base-case valuation without order conversion.

### 8.4 Order book and order intake

Order book gives near-term visibility. Outstanding order book was Rs 22.20 billion at Q2 FY26 and Rs 19.86 billion at Q3 FY26.

Positive factors:

- near-term revenue visibility
- exports and domestic inquiry pipeline
- customer demand resilience

Risks:

- order-book conversion timing
- order cancellations or deferrals
- margin quality of new orders

DCF treatment:

- Order book supports near-term revenue.
- Longer-term valuation still depends on repeat order intake.

## 10. WACC build-up

### Base WACC: 12.0%

Reasoning:

- Triveni Turbine is a high-quality Indian capital-goods company.
- Business quality, strong margins and likely low leverage support a lower risk premium than midcap cyclicals.
- Export and order-book lumpiness justify a company-specific risk premium.
- The valuation premium and capital-goods cyclicality prevent using a too-low WACC.

### Bear WACC: 14.0%

Used when:

- export demand weakens,
- order conversion slows,
- margins normalize,
- global capex slows,
- investors demand higher capital-goods risk premium.

### Bull WACC: 10.5%

Used when:

- export and aftermarket growth sustain,
- cash conversion remains strong,
- order book grows with healthy margins,
- market confidence improves.

## 13. Literature-based review

### Damodaran lens

- High-quality growth companies still need valuation discipline.
- FCFF should reflect working capital and order execution, not just order book.
- Relative valuation is useful but can mislead if peers are also expensive.

### Fisher lens

- Triveni has several Fisher-style qualities: engineering capability, niche leadership, export runway and aftermarket opportunity.
- The key qualitative question is whether management can compound through international markets and service revenue.

### Basant Maheshwari / Indian market lens

- Quality plus growth can justify a premium, but entry price remains critical.
- P/E expansion may already be priced in after strong capital-goods performance.
- Order book and ROCE matter, but cash conversion must validate growth.

### Malkiel lens

- Avoid overconfidence in projecting order-book conversion and market-share gains.
- Compare against passive benchmarks and capital-goods sector performance.
- Do not assume recent capital-goods momentum continues indefinitely.

### Palomar / portfolio lens

- Triveni adds capital-goods exposure and export/renewable industrial theme exposure.
- Position size should reflect valuation premium and possible drawdown from multiple compression.

### Stochastic DCF lens

- Scenario analysis is appropriate because future cash flows depend on uncertain order conversion, exports, margins and working capital.
- A future Monte Carlo model could vary order-book conversion, EBITDA margins and WACC.

## 15. Final conclusion v1

Triveni Turbine is one of the strongest quality businesses in the project universe. It has strong margins, export growth, aftermarket contribution, renewable/waste-heat exposure and a robust order book. It deserves a place on a long-term watchlist.

However, at the current price proxy, the base-case DCF does not provide the required 25% margin of safety. The stock becomes attractive only if the market price corrects closer to the MOS zone or if further evidence shows that export growth, aftermarket mix and order conversion can lift normalized FCFF above the current base case.

### Final view

> Watchlist / High-Quality Capital Goods Compounder — Not a base-case margin-of-safety candidate at current price. Revisit after FY26 audited annual report, full-year cash flow, order-book conversion and export/aftermarket mix update.
