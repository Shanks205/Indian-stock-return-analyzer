## 1. Executive summary

Force Motors is an Indian automobile manufacturer with exposure to vans, multi-utility vehicles, light commercial vehicles, shared mobility, specialised mobility solutions and high-tech aggregate / engine manufacturing. The company is known for products such as Traveller and Urbania, and also participates in contract engine and aggregate manufacturing for premium OEMs.

Force Motors is different from the earlier portfolio companies because it combines an OEM vehicle business with high-tech aggregate manufacturing. Its valuation depends on domestic commercial/passenger carrier demand, Traveller/Urbania volumes, operating leverage, engine manufacturing programs, premium OEM relationships, capex intensity, working capital and margin sustainability.

The public-source v1 thesis is that Force Motors should be modelled as a **cyclical but improving auto/OEM and engine-manufacturing company with strong recent profitability, operating leverage, debt reduction, customer/program concentration and capex-cycle risk**.

The DCF should not simply extrapolate exceptional quarterly profit or one-off gains. A normalized operating-margin approach is more appropriate.

## 4. Investment thesis

Force Motors' investment thesis is based on five pillars:

| Pillar | Research view |
|---|---|
| Strong recent execution | Q3 FY26 and 9M FY26 showed record revenue, EBITDA and profitability. |
| Operating leverage | Profit growth exceeded revenue growth, showing strong margin leverage. |
| Product franchise | Traveller and Urbania provide identifiable product platforms. |
| Engine / aggregate business | Premium OEM engine/aggregate programs provide diversification and quality perception. |
| Normalization risk | Exceptional items and strong cycle margins should not be treated as permanent. |

The base case should use normalized operating performance and moderate growth. The bull case can capture stronger Urbania adoption, mobility demand, high-tech aggregate growth and sustained operating leverage. The bear case should reflect auto-cycle slowdown, program concentration, raw-material inflation and margin normalization.

## 6. Financial performance review

Force Motors announced strong Q3 FY26 and 9M FY26 standalone results. The official company release reported Q3 FY26 revenue of around ₹2,155 crore, up 13% year-on-year, and 9M FY26 revenue of around ₹6,583 crore, up 14% year-on-year. Q3 FY26 EBITDA was around ₹401 crore, up 63% year-on-year, while 9M FY26 EBITDA was around ₹1,145 crore, up 43% year-on-year. PBT before exceptional items was around ₹328 crore in Q3 FY26 and ₹931 crore in 9M FY26.

PAT after exceptional items was around ₹403 crore in Q3 FY26 and ₹938 crore in 9M FY26. The company also reported nil total debt in the Q3 FY26 release.

The correct research treatment is:

> Force Motors' Q3/9M FY26 operating momentum is strong, but the DCF must separate recurring operating performance from exceptional gains and avoid assuming peak margins permanently.

## 9. WACC framework

### Cost of equity

```text
Cost of Equity = Risk-free Rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium + Auto/OEM Execution Risk Premium
```

An auto/OEM execution risk premium is useful because reported beta may not fully capture platform concentration, product-cycle risk, customer/program dependence, capex requirements and margin normalization risk.

### WACC

```text
WACC = Equity Weight × Cost of Equity + Debt Weight × After-tax Cost of Debt
```

Force Motors' DCF is most sensitive to:

- vehicle demand growth
- EBITDA margin sustainability
- operating leverage
- high-tech aggregate program growth
- capex intensity
- working-capital cycle
- WACC
- terminal growth
