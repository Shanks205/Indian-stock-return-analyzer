## 1. Executive summary

Hindustan Zinc Limited is one of India’s largest integrated zinc, lead and silver producers. It is majority-owned by Vedanta and has significant commodity exposure through zinc, lead and silver prices.

Hindustan Zinc is a different kind of commodity case from NALCO. NALCO is linked to aluminium and alumina economics, while Hindustan Zinc is tied to zinc, lead and silver cycles. Silver can be a major profit driver and can create sharp earnings upside during precious-metal upcycles.

The public-source v1 thesis is that Hindustan Zinc should be modelled as a **high-margin, commodity-linked, dividend-relevant metals company with strong current profitability but meaningful cycle risk**.

The DCF should not blindly extrapolate strong zinc/silver prices. A mid-cycle margin approach is more appropriate.

## 2. Business overview

Hindustan Zinc operates across an integrated zinc-lead-silver mining and smelting value chain.

| Business area | Role in thesis |
|---|---|
| Zinc mining and smelting | Core revenue and earnings driver |
| Lead production | Secondary base-metal contribution |
| Silver production | Important precious-metal by-product and upside driver |
| Captive mines | Resource security and cost advantage |
| Power / energy systems | Important cost factor for smelting operations |
| Expansion and mining-life projects | Long-term resource and production sustainability |

The integrated mining structure supports cost competitiveness, but earnings remain highly sensitive to metal prices, USD/INR, energy cost, grade, volumes and mining/smelting efficiency.

## 3. Industry context

Zinc demand is linked to steel galvanization, construction, infrastructure, automotive and industrial production. Silver demand is linked to jewellery, investment, solar, electronics and industrial applications. Lead demand is linked to batteries and industrial use.

Important industry variables for Hindustan Zinc:

- zinc prices
- silver prices
- lead prices
- mine grades and volumes
- treatment/refining economics
- USD/INR movement
- global zinc concentrate supply
- Chinese demand and supply conditions

- royalty and mining levies

- dividend expectations

## 4. Investment thesis

Hindustan Zinc's investment thesis is based on five pillars:

| Pillar | Research view |
|---|---|
| Integrated resource base | Captive mining and integrated smelting support cost competitiveness. |
| Strong recent profitability | FY26 has benefited from better metal prices, especially zinc and silver. |
| Silver optionality | Silver can materially support earnings during precious-metal upcycles. |
| Dividend relevance | Hindustan Zinc is often viewed as a dividend-relevant commodity stock. |
| Commodity-cycle risk | Valuation must normalize margins and not extrapolate peak prices. |

The base case should use normalized metal-cycle assumptions. The bull case can capture stronger zinc and silver prices, higher volumes and improved cost performance. The bear case should capture metal-price correction, cost inflation and weaker margins.

## 6. Financial performance review

Hindustan Zinc reported a strong Q3 FY26. Public result coverage reported Q3 FY26 consolidated net profit of around ₹3,916 crore, up 46% year-on-year, and revenue from operations around ₹10,627 crore, up 27.8% year-on-year. Performance was supported by higher production, stronger zinc and silver prices, cost performance, currency tailwinds and better by-product realizations.

S&P Global also noted that Hindustan Zinc was on track for a strong FY26, supported significantly by silver-price strength.

The correct research treatment is:

> Hindustan Zinc's current earnings strength is real, but the DCF must normalize zinc/silver cycle assumptions rather than treat a strong metals cycle as permanent.

## 7. DCF assumption justification

| DCF input | Base-case treatment | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue base | FY26E based on quarterly run-rate and public FY26 estimates | FY26 has strong metal-price support; full-year data requires audited reconciliation. |
| Revenue growth | Low-to-moderate normalized growth | Volume growth is limited by mining capacity and grades; revenue also depends heavily on metal prices. |
| EBITDA / EBIT margin | Normalized below peak-cycle levels | Current zinc/silver pricing may not persist forever. |
| Depreciation | Linked to mine and smelter asset base | Mining and smelting are capital-intensive. |
| Capex | Moderate to high | Mine development, reserve replacement, smelting, power and sustainability capex are required. |
| Working capital | Conservative | Metal inventory and price-cycle movements can affect working capital. |
| Tax / royalty | Normalized effective burden | Mining royalties and tax treatment require reconciliation. |
| Terminal growth | Conservative | Commodity businesses should not be valued with aggressive perpetual growth. |
| Net debt / cash | Added separately | Balance sheet must be reconciled from annual report and filings. |

## 8. Scenario logic

### Bear case

- Zinc and silver prices correct sharply
- Energy and input costs rise
- Mining grades disappoint
- Royalty/tax burden increases
- Dividend expectations reduce future reinvestment flexibility
- Global demand weakens

### Base case

- FY26 remains strong but margins normalize
- Zinc and silver prices moderate from strong levels
- Volumes remain stable
- Cost efficiency supports healthy mid-cycle margins
- Capex remains manageable

### Bull case

- Zinc and silver prices remain elevated
- Silver contribution expands meaningfully
- Volumes and mine performance improve
- Cost efficiency supports stronger margins
- Dividend profile remains attractive

## 9. WACC framework

### Cost of equity

```text
Cost of Equity = Risk-free Rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium + Commodity / Ownership Risk Premium
```

A commodity / ownership risk premium is useful because Hindustan Zinc's observed beta may not fully capture zinc/silver price risk, dividend expectations, ownership structure, mining policy and capital-allocation complexity.

### WACC

```text
WACC = Equity Weight × Cost of Equity + Debt Weight × After-tax Cost of Debt
```

Hindustan Zinc's DCF is most sensitive to:

- zinc and silver price assumptions
- normalized EBITDA margin
- WACC
- terminal growth
- capex intensity
- dividend / capital allocation policy
