## 1. Executive summary

Triveni Turbine is an Indian capital-goods company focused on industrial steam turbines and related energy solutions. It serves domestic and export markets across industrial power generation, renewable energy, biomass, waste-to-energy, process industries and aftermarket/service opportunities.

Triveni Turbine is different from the commodity and auto-cycle cases because it is primarily an **order-book, execution, export and margin-quality capital-goods case**. The main valuation debate is whether the company can sustain high margins, strong exports, aftermarket contribution and order-book conversion without overcapitalizing one strong quarter.

The public-source v1 thesis is that Triveni Turbine should be modelled as a **high-quality industrial turbine company with strong export momentum, healthy order book, high EBITDA margins, aftermarket optionality and valuation sensitivity to order inflow and execution**.

The DCF should not simply extrapolate peak margins permanently. A normalized margin and order-book conversion approach is more appropriate.

## 4. Investment thesis

Triveni Turbine's investment thesis is based on five pillars:

| Pillar | Research view |
|---|---|
| Strong order book | Q3 FY26 outstanding order book of around ₹1,986 crore supports revenue visibility. |
| Export momentum | Export sales grew strongly in Q3 FY26 and represented a high share of sales. |
| Margin quality | EBITDA margin remained high at around 24.6% in Q3 FY26. |
| Aftermarket optionality | Services and aftermarket can support margin resilience and recurring revenue. |
| Valuation discipline | High-quality capital-goods businesses can be expensive; DCF should normalize margins and order inflow. |

The base case should use healthy but disciplined growth and margin assumptions. The bull case can capture stronger export demand, aftermarket mix and energy-transition order inflow. The bear case should reflect order slowdown, margin normalization, execution delays and working-capital stretch.

## 6. Financial performance review

Triveni Turbine reported its highest-ever quarterly revenue and EBITDA in Q3 FY26. The company investor page reported Q3 FY26 revenue from operations of ₹6.24 billion, EBITDA of ₹1.54 billion, PBT of ₹1.44 billion, PAT of ₹917 million and outstanding order book of ₹19.86 billion. External result coverage also reported Q3 FY26 revenue of about ₹624 crore, EBITDA of about ₹154 crore and EBITDA margin of around 24.6%.

For 9M FY26, public result coverage reported revenue from operations of around ₹1,501.5 crore, EBITDA of around ₹382.4 crore and PAT of around ₹247.5 crore. Order book remained healthy, although order booking can vary quarter to quarter.

The correct research treatment is:

> Triveni Turbine's Q3 FY26 performance confirms strong execution and export momentum, but the DCF must normalize order inflow, execution timing and margins before treating current profitability as permanent.

## 8. Scenario logic

### Bear case

- Order inflow slows
- Export growth normalizes
- EBITDA margins compress from high levels
- Execution delays affect revenue conversion
- Working capital absorbs cash
- Competition increases pricing pressure
- Aftermarket growth disappoints

### Base case

- Order book converts steadily
- Export momentum remains healthy but normalizes
- EBITDA margins stay strong but below peak levels
- Aftermarket/services support margin quality
- Capex remains manageable
- Working capital remains controlled

### Bull case

- Export orders accelerate
- Energy-transition applications create strong demand
- Aftermarket contribution rises materially
- Operating leverage sustains high margins
- Order book expands and converts efficiently
- Cash conversion remains strong

## 9. WACC framework

### Cost of equity

```text
Cost of Equity = Risk-free Rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium + Capital-Goods / Export Execution Risk Premium
```

A capital-goods / export execution risk premium is useful because reported beta may not fully capture order-book cyclicality, export execution, margin normalization and working-capital risk.

### WACC

```text
WACC = Equity Weight × Cost of Equity + Debt Weight × After-tax Cost of Debt
```

Triveni Turbine's DCF is most sensitive to:

- order inflow and conversion
- export revenue growth
- EBITDA margin sustainability
- aftermarket mix
- working-capital cycle
- WACC
- terminal growth
