# Natco Pharma Assumptions Log

## Research version

**Version:** Public-Source Complete v1 — Institutional-Style Preliminary Valuation  
**Company:** Natco Pharma Ltd.  
**Valuation date:** 29 April 2026  
**Purpose:** Document the logic behind the major valuation assumptions.

---

## Assumption discipline

Natco should not be valued by simply annualizing a strong quarter or a strong product cycle.

Every assumption should consider:

- product concentration
- export formulation volatility
- price erosion
- regulatory risk
- domestic specialty growth
- pipeline optionality
- net-cash balance sheet strength
- normalized rather than peak profitability

---

## Revenue base

### Base-case logic

Use a normalized FY26E / FY27E base rather than FY25 peak revenue as a permanent growth base.

### Why

FY25 was a strong year, while FY26 quarterly performance shows earnings normalization and volatility. A conservative model should allow a reset before moderate recovery.

### Modelling rule

Do not treat one-off high-margin product cycles as recurring forever.

---

## Revenue growth

### Base-case logic

Model a reset followed by moderate recovery.

### Why

Growth may be supported by domestic formulations, oncology, export launches, and pipeline products. However, US generics pricing, product concentration, and launch timing can create large volatility.

---

## EBITDA / EBIT margin

### Base-case logic

Use normalized margins below FY25 peak levels.

### Why

FY25 profitability was unusually high. Natco's long-term margin should reflect both high-value product opportunities and inevitable price erosion / competition.

### Modelling rule

Margin upside should be stronger in the bull case, not automatically embedded in the base case.

---

## Tax rate

### Base-case logic

Use a normalized effective tax rate.

### Why

Pharma tax rates can vary due to geography, incentives, subsidiaries, and one-off items.

### Refresh requirement

Reconcile with latest audited annual report and quarterly results.

---

## Depreciation and amortization

### Base-case logic

Link depreciation to revenue and manufacturing asset intensity.

### Why

Natco requires manufacturing plants, compliance infrastructure, and R&D/manufacturing assets.

---

## Capital expenditure

### Base-case logic

Use moderate to high capex intensity relative to sales.

### Why

Capex is needed for manufacturing capacity, regulated-market compliance, product launches, and pipeline execution.

---

## Working capital

### Base-case logic

Use conservative working-capital normalization.

### Why

Pharma inventories, receivables, export cycles, and product-launch timing can create working-capital swings.

---

## Terminal growth

### Base-case logic

Use conservative terminal growth.

### Why

Long-term value should reflect the normalized business, not permanent high-value launch economics.

---

## WACC

### Required inputs

- India 10-year government bond yield
- regression beta vs Nifty Pharma / Nifty 50
- India equity risk premium
- company-specific risk premium
- cost of debt
- effective tax rate
- market value of equity
- cash and debt balances

### Current status

Framework complete for v1. Numerical WACC uses a company-specific risk premium because low observed beta may understate product, regulatory, and pipeline risk.

## Scenario assumption summary

| Assumption | Bear case | Base case | Bull case |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue growth | Weak recovery | Reset then moderate recovery | Strong pipeline-led recovery |
| Margin | Faster compression | Normalized below peak | Stabilizes above base |
| Capex | Higher burden | Moderate to high | Productive growth capex |
| Working capital | Less efficient | Normalized | Better efficiency |
| Terminal growth | Conservative | Moderate | Slightly higher but disciplined |
| WACC | Higher | Normalized with company risk premium | Lower / stable |

## Final note

This assumptions log should be updated whenever the DCF model changes. The key analytical discipline for Natco is normalization: the analyst must separate durable earnings from product-cycle windfalls.
