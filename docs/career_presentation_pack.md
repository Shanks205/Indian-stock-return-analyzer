## 7. Detailed interview explanation

### What problem were you solving?

Most beginner stock projects stop at returns, charts or simple ratios. I wanted to build a more complete finance project that resembles an analyst workflow: company research, valuation assumptions, peer framework, risk analysis and data-quality discipline.

### What did you build?

I built a 10-company Indian Equity Valuation Lab with:

- company-level research notes
- FCFF DCF logic
- WACC frameworks
- scenario and sensitivity analysis
- peer-comparison frameworks
- risk matrices
- assumptions logs
- source logs
- completion audits
- consolidated valuation dashboard
- Excel workbook pack
- Python automation scripts

### Why is it useful?

It demonstrates how to think like a financial analyst, not only how to calculate ratios. It shows sector-specific judgement: each company needs different valuation logic depending on business model, cyclicality, margin drivers and risk profile.

### What makes it credible?

The project separates completed public-source research from future audited-data refresh requirements. It does not falsely claim to be fully institutional-grade published research. It clearly records which assumptions are preliminary and which require audited annual-report reconciliation.

### What would you improve next?

The next stage would be audited FY26 data refresh, beta regression execution, date-consistent peer tables, market-price update and final valuation range recalculation.

## 12. Interview Q&A preparation

### Q1. Why did you choose these companies?

I selected companies across different sectors so that the project would test different valuation methods and business models. For example, metals companies require commodity-cycle normalization, CPCL requires refining-margin analysis, Natco requires pipeline and product-cycle thinking, and Triveni Turbine requires order-book analysis.

### Q2. Why did you use DCF?

DCF helps connect business assumptions to intrinsic value. It forces the analyst to think about revenue growth, margins, reinvestment, working capital, risk and terminal value. I used peer comparison as a sanity check rather than a replacement for DCF.

### Q3. What was the hardest part?

The hardest part was avoiding over-extrapolation. Many companies had strong recent quarterly results, but a professional valuation needs normalized assumptions. I had to separate sustainable earnings power from cyclical or one-off performance.

### Q4. What makes this project different from a basic stock analysis project?

It is structured like a research process, not just a return chart. Every company has an assumptions log, source log, risk matrix, peer framework and completion audit. I also built Python/Excel automation so the project can be refreshed systematically.

### Q5. Is this investment advice?

No. It is a research and learning project. I clearly label it as Public-Source Complete v1 and disclose that final institutional-grade use would require audited data refresh, market-data validation, peer numerical tables and independent review.
